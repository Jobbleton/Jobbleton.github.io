Reflection

Where to begin? On reflecting over my three and a half years at Iowa State University (ISU), one must put the whole experience into context. As a married adult with two kids of my own before even starting at ISU, my experience was unique in many aspects. Although different from most other students, one thing for sure is similar – I experienced growth, and lots of it.

Before coming to ISU I worked professionally in the Information Technology (IT) field. I have always been interested in technology, but I wanted to be a producer of technology, rather than just a consumer (or supporter in IT). So when the company I was working with was purchased and moved out of state, I figured it was the opportune time to make that transition.

Having programmed some for my IT tasks, I felt that Computer Science would be the appropriate place to start. Therefore, in spring of 2004 I started in the Liberal Arts and Sciences College, in pre-Computer Science. Right away I got involved with a lab which would greatly affect the rest of my time at ISU. The Space System and Controls Lab (SSCL) is a multi-disciplinary lab that got started by launching high-altitude balloon experiments. My initial role with the lab was computer support. My time was cut short, however, because after one semester I stopped going to school.

I was shocked in my first semester by how much education cost. I figured that I could not afford to get my degree. So I took a 3 year hiatus trying to find other work in IT. Over those three years I found work in just about everything except IT. Ultimately, my break taught me that I couldn’t afford not to get my degree.

In spring of 2007 I came back to ISU, trying to continue where I left off. In my return I was able to see the progression of the technical track I was following, how the faculty was working on staying updated, and how important it was to stay current with modern technology. I immediately started working for the SSCL lab again, but this time I pushed to get more involved by taking on various programming projects within the lab. Over the next three years I would write three main programs for the lab, each with a very distinct purpose.

The first project for the SSCL lab I started was a flight and logistics program used to track the high-altitude balloon experiments. This software required me to work hand-in-hand with engineers from other disciplines to make sure the large software package provided them the information, tools, and data logging they needed. Through participating in the operations of those experiments, I also learned valuable organizational skills and the importance of keeping a professional relationship with external organizations such as local airports and the Federal Aviation Administration.

The second project for the SSCL was control software for the lab’s Autonomous Jeep (AJ). Once again, to be successful I needed to work closely with other engineers. This time I implemented a control algorithm to drive a radio controlled jeep as a test-bed for an aerospace graduate student. Around this time I also realized that knowing how to design the software was not enough – I wanted to learn how to make the electronics it interfaced with. So I switched majors to Electrical Engineering, with a minor in Computer Science.

The third project for the SSCL lab was to design a LabView program to control a Venus atmospheric simulation chamber. This project opened my eyes to a unique programming style, and a whole new level of safety concerns. By writing software and modifying the hardware that would need to control a chamber running at 500 degrees Celsius and 1400 Psi, I would learn the my responsibility as an engineer to be proficient enough at my trade, and methodical enough following good engineering practices to keep others around the chamber safe and still complete the experiments successfully. It was through this project that I eventually travelled to the Goddard NASA center in Maryland to train the chamber’s new owners how to use it.

My time in the SSCL taught me much of my own engineering discipline, as well as mechanical and aerospace from the excellent examples of my peers. In working to provide real solutions, I realized that some solutions had to be quick, others were as simple as using Velcro instead of a screw, yet others required the courage to do a major system overhaul. Exposure to so many different types of problems and their solutions will inevitably help me solve more complicated problems in the future.

Amidst my work at the SSCL I was under a constant barrage of math and science problems from my classwork. Such repetition, when combined with hands-on problem solving, went a long way towards forming a solid mental link from theory to practice. Without what I was learning in these classes, I would not have been able to provide many of the solutions. Also, in learning more and more of the supporting theory behind the solutions I was providing, I hungered for the frontier of our collective knowledge. In my graduate level classes in Computational Perception and Reconfigurable Computing, I have been able to feed that hunger. Providing solutions to modern problems will certainly be helped by these upcoming technologies.

More than just the classes, my experience at ISU has been affected through friendships gained, my successes, my failures, and exposure to so much. In the end, the greatest thing it has taught me is what I am capable of. When put under considerable pressure, and in foreign circumstances, I persevered and in so doing, realized a level of inner commitment that I didn’t know of before.
